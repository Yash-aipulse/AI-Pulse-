Anthropic is raising up to $50 billion at a valuation that would make it the most valuable AI startup in the world, as its revenue has grown fivefold in roughly five months

Anthropic, the company that makes the Claude AI assistant, is in discussions to raise up to $50 billion from outside investors at a pre-money valuation of approximately $900 billion – above the $852 billion valuation set by OpenAI, its closest competitor, in March 2026. The round is being led by CFO Krishna Rao, with interested investors including Dragoneer Investment Group, General Catalyst, and Lightspeed Venture Partners. Sources close to the process told TechCrunch it could close within two weeks. Anthropic has publicly confirmed that its annualised revenue has passed $30 billion; sources cited by the Financial Times place the current figure closer to $40 to $45 billion, driven primarily by Claude Code, a coding tool, and Cowork, a workplace productivity product. At the end of 2025, Anthropic's annualised revenue was $9 billion. The round is expected to be the company's last private fundraise before a potential stock market listing targeted for late 2026.

Why it mattersRevenue growing fivefold in roughly five months is not a trend line – it is a discontinuity. The $900 billion valuation is the financial market's attempt to price that rate of acceleration forward. For any enterprise that has been deferring a commitment to an AI vendor, the pace at which Anthropic is embedding itself into production environments is shortening that window.

For enterprise technology buyers

Meta released its first closed, proprietary flagship AI model – breaking from its open-source strategy – while confirming it will spend up to $135 billion on AI infrastructure this year

Meta, the company that owns Facebook, Instagram, and WhatsApp, unveiled a new AI model called Muse Spark, developed under a newly formed internal unit called Superintelligence Labs. Muse Spark is proprietary – meaning it is not freely available for anyone to download and use, which is a significant departure from Meta's previous strategy of releasing its Llama models as open-source software that any organisation could use without paying. The new model reportedly performs competitively on tasks involving reasoning, image understanding, and autonomous task completion, at a lower computing cost than Meta's own mid-size Llama 4 variants. Separately, Meta confirmed it will spend between $115 billion and $135 billion on AI infrastructure in 2026, roughly double what it spent last year.

Why it mattersMeta's open-source Llama models became popular with developers and enterprises precisely because they cost nothing and created no vendor dependency. Closing the door on that approach for its best-performing model tier changes the terms of that relationship. Any organisation that built its AI strategy around free or low-cost access to Meta's frontier models needs to reassess that dependency.

Six national cybersecurity agencies jointly warned that AI agents are already operating inside critical infrastructure with access levels that organisations cannot safely monitor

On May 1, 2026, the cybersecurity agencies of all five Five Eyes nations – CISA and the NSA from the United States, the UK's National Cyber Security Centre, Australia's Australian Signals Directorate, Canada's Centre for Cyber Security, and New Zealand's National Cyber Security Centre – published a joint 30-page guidance document titled "Careful Adoption of Agentic AI Services." An AI agent is an AI system that can take actions autonomously – browsing the web, sending emails, running software, or moving files – rather than simply answering questions. The document identifies five categories of risk: agents being granted access far beyond what their task requires; design and configuration flaws; agents pursuing goals in ways their operators didn't intend; failures cascading across networks of interconnected agents; and audit logs that are hard to interpret or have been altered. The guidance is directly tied to a confirmed incident in April 2026, in which an adversary used a commercial AI model to navigate security barriers inside the computer systems controlling a municipal water utility in Monterrey, Mexico. The agencies recommend that organisations treat agents as untrusted components until proper governance is in place, use short-lived access credentials, and begin any deployment with low-risk tasks only.

Why it mattersThis is not advisory guidance from a think tank – it is coordinated policy from the national security agencies of five governments, anchored to a real attack on public infrastructure. Agentic AI governance has moved from an emerging risk category to a national security priority in the span of one document.

Enterprises in financial services, healthcare, and government will increasingly require vendors to demonstrate compliance with these five risk categories before signing a contract. Build your compliance documentation against these categories now, so you are not assembling it under time pressure when a customer asks.

Canada's Cohere is acquiring Germany's Aleph Alpha in a deal backed by Europe's largest retailer, creating the first transatlantic AI company positioned as a sovereign alternative to US and Chinese providers

Cohere, a Canadian enterprise AI company, announced on April 24 that it is merging with Aleph Alpha, a German AI company that holds contracts with the German federal government and regional authorities. The merged company will keep the Cohere name and maintain offices in both Toronto and Berlin. The deal is anchored by a €500 million (approximately $600 million) investment from Schwarz Group, the German parent company of the Lidl and Kaufland supermarket chains and Europe's largest retailer. The combined entity is valued at approximately $20 billion. Cohere shareholders will receive approximately 90% of the merged company; Aleph Alpha shareholders the remaining 10%. The announcement was attended by the Digital Ministers of both Canada and Germany, giving it an explicit government endorsement that most private M&A deals do not carry. The strategic pitch is direct: European enterprises and governments that need their data to remain subject to European law – and independent from the US Cloud Act – now have a scaled, commercially viable AI option with government-backed credibility. Cohere reported $240 million in annual recurring revenue in 2025.

Why it mattersSovereign AI has moved from a policy talking point to an investable, government-endorsed commercial structure. For European organisations that have been deferring AI procurement decisions while waiting for a credible non-American option, this deal is the most concrete candidate yet.

JPMorgan Chase reclassified its AI spending from optional innovation into the same budget category as fraud detection and payment systems

JPMorgan Chase, the largest bank in the world by assets, has moved its AI investment out of its discretionary research-and-development budget and into its non-discretionary operating baseline – the category that also contains cybersecurity infrastructure and core payment rails. The bank's total technology budget for 2026 is $19.8 billion, of which approximately $2 billion is now designated as AI investment. CEO Jamie Dimon has stated publicly that AI has already generated approximately $2 billion in operational savings, making the investment broadly self-funding. JPMorgan has approximately 2,000 employees dedicated to AI development. The reclassification has a direct governance consequence: spending classified as core infrastructure is subject to the same model risk standards, auditability requirements, and regulatory oversight as other non-discretionary systems – it cannot be paused or cut in a budget cycle without the same justification required to cut fraud monitoring.

Why it mattersWhen the largest bank in the world reclassifies AI from experiment to infrastructure, the signal moves downstream to every other financial institution in its competitive tier. Banks that still treat AI as a project-based initiative – with project-based budgets and commitments that can be reversed – are now making an explicit structural bet against their largest competitor.

A US federal court ruled that platforms whose AI systems exercise creative authority over advertising content lose their standard legal immunity from liability – creating new exposure for Meta, Google, Snap, TikTok, and X

A federal judge in California has denied Meta the legal protection it sought in two related cases involving fraudulent investment advertisements. The protection Meta sought is called Section 230 immunity – a provision of US law that generally shields online platforms from legal responsibility for content posted by third-party users, on the grounds that the platform is a passive host rather than the author of that content. In the first case, Bouck v. Meta Platforms, the court found that Meta's AI advertising tools had themselves shaped the final content of fraudulent investment solicitations, making Meta a co-developer of the fraud rather than a passive host. In the second case, Suddeth v. Meta Platforms, fraudsters purchased ad placements, uploaded fabricated endorsements using licensed financial advisers' names and photographs, and directed victims to private messaging groups. One promoted stock lost 90% of its value within minutes of the campaign running. The court's reasoning is that standard securities fraud law – specifically a provision called Rule 10b-5 – has no equivalent of Section 230 protection. Any platform whose AI systems exercise authority over the assembled final content of an investment solicitation may be considered the legal maker of that statement, regardless of whether the platform intended to commit fraud. The judge identified Alphabet (Google's parent company), Snap, TikTok, and X Corp as facing the same exposure under the same legal framework.

Why it mattersSection 230 has been the legal foundation on which every major platform built its advertising business model. The court's reasoning does not require proving the platform intended fraud – only that its AI exercised creative authority over the content. That is a normal description of how AI-powered advertising optimisation systems function today.

Review current contracts and flag any that do not contain this language before the next renewal.

For financial services and investment firms advertising digitally

Fraudsters are using your licensed advisers' names, headshots, and branding in AI-assembled advertisements without your knowledge. The Head of Compliance should assess, within the next 30 days, whether your firm has any monitoring in place to detect unauthorised use of your name, logo, or personnel imagery across major advertising platforms. Identifying this exposure before a regulatory inquiry is significantly less costly than discovering it during one.

Editor's note
Six stories, one structural pattern: the risk layer around AI is catching up with the capability layer, and the institutions sitting between the two are running out of time to prepare.

Anthropic raising at a $900 billion valuation is a market-formation event – it sets a reference price that cascades across every competing deal and vendor negotiation in enterprise AI. Meta closing its best model signals the end of the assumption that frontier capability will remain free. The Five Eyes guidance acknowledges in official government language what security teams already know: agentic AI is inside critical infrastructure and nobody audited its access rights. Cohere and Aleph Alpha are betting that regulatory legitimacy – sovereign data, GDPR compliance, Cloud Act independence – is worth as much as model performance in the European and government markets. JPMorgan's reclassification sends a signal to every competitor with a smaller balance sheet: AI is now baseline operating cost, not optional investment. And the Bouck ruling establishes that the same AI optimisation engines driving platform advertising revenue may simultaneously be creating securities fraud liability.

The organisations most exposed are those that moved quickly enough in 2024 to have AI in production – but not carefully enough to have governance, legal review, or security controls that match what is now expected. The standards have moved. The pilots have not.
