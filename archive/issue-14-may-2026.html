Cisco is cutting nearly 4,000 jobs while reporting record quarterly revenue of $15.8 billion and $5.3 billion in AI infrastructure orders, funding its AI build-out by reducing its own headcount

Cisco Systems, the US company that makes the networking hardware and software that connects corporate computer systems, announced on May 14 it will eliminate ~4,000 jobs – roughly 5% of its global workforce. The cuts were disclosed alongside fiscal Q3 2026 earnings that set a company revenue record at $15.8 billion, up 12% Y-o-Y from $14.15 billion in the same quarter last year. Cisco said it has booked $5.3 billion in AI infrastructure orders from hyperscalers (the large data-center operators such as Microsoft, Amazon, Google, and Meta) year-to-date, and raised its full-year AI orders target to ~$9 billion, up from a prior estimate of $5 billion. Revised FY2026 AI segment revenue guidance was lifted to ~$4 billion from $3 billion.

The restructuring is expected to cost up to $1 billion, primarily in severance. CEO Chuck Robbins described the move publicly as a "cost structure" reset to redirect capital toward silicon, optics, security, and AI infrastructure – not a response to falling demand. CEO Robbins was disclosed in public SEC filings to have earned more than $52 million in executive compensation during 2025; a Cisco spokesperson did not confirm whether his compensation would be reduced in connection with the layoffs. Cisco has now conducted staff reductions repeatedly since 2024.

Why it mattersThis is the clearest example yet of a large technology incumbent funding its AI capacity build by shrinking its own workforce while simultaneously posting record financial results. It reframes "AI investment" not as additional spending layered on top of existing operations, but as a direct substitution of payroll for compute, silicon, and networking equipment sold into hyperscaler buildouts. Other networking and security incumbents will face board pressure to follow the same template.

PwC and Anthropic are expanding their strategic alliance to train 30,000 US staff on Claude and target more than $2 trillion in enterprise technical debt across Fortune 500 companies

PwC, one of the Big 4 global professional services firms, and Anthropic announced on May 14 a major expansion of their existing strategic alliance. PwC will deploy Claude Code and Claude Cowork (Anthropic's coding and workplace productivity tools) starting with US teams and extending toward its global workforce of more than 364,000 people across 136 countries and 137 territories. The deal includes a joint Center of Excellence and a commitment to train and certify 30,000 US PwC professionals on Claude. The two firms explicitly framed the partnership as targeting "more than $2 trillion in technical debt within companies' operations" – referring to the cost of maintaining and running aging computer systems rather than replacing them with modern alternatives. Research from McKinsey, cited in the announcement, estimates that large companies spend 10–20% of their technology budgets simply keeping legacy systems running.

The expanded alliance focuses on 3 areas: building agentic technology (AI systems that can take actions autonomously) using Claude Code; AI-native deal advisory covering due diligence, value creation, and integration; and reinventing finance, supply chain, HR, and engineering operating models. PwC is launching a new Claude-native finance business group inside its Office of the CFO practice. Named production deployments include insurance underwriting cycles compressed from 10 weeks to 10 days, an HR transformation prototype delivered in one week, and cybersecurity incident response moving from hours to minutes. Clients are reporting delivery improvements of up to 70%, per the PwC release. Advocate Health, one of the largest US health systems, is named as an organization building toward full-scale deployment across its 167,000-person workforce.

Why it mattersThe Big 4 consulting firms have historically been the implementation layer for every major technology transition – ERP, cloud, and digital transformation. By committing PwC's entire workforce to a single frontier-model vendor at scale, this deal effectively makes Anthropic the default AI substrate for a significant share of Fortune 500 modernization spend over the next three years. It also puts competitive pressure on every other major consulting firm, which will now face the same "10 weeks to 10 days" benchmark in client conversations.

Defense technology company Anduril raised $5 billion at a $61 billion valuation – more than double its mid-2025 value – as the US Department of Defense simultaneously agreed to buy more than 10,000 hypersonic missiles from a new group of software-first suppliers

Anduril Industries, a US defense technology company that builds AI-enabled autonomous weapons systems and defense software, announced on May 13 a $5 billion Series H funding round at a $61 billion post-money valuation, led by returning investors Thrive Capital and Andreessen Horowitz. The round more than doubles the company's mid-2025 valuation of $30.5 billion – itself a large increase from its $4.7 billion valuation in 2021. CEO Brian Schimpf stated that Anduril doubled its revenue in 2025 to $2.2 billion, and will invest the new capital aggressively in manufacturing capacity, research and development, and infrastructure.

The fundraise landed in the same week the US Department of Defense announced an agreement to acquire more than 10,000 low-cost hypersonic missiles over the next three years from a group including Anduril, CoAspire, Leidos, and Zone 5 – signaling the DoD's willingness to buy from multiple new software-centric suppliers rather than a single traditional defense contractor. Anduril has also confirmed contracts for the US "Golden Dome" space-based missile defense initiative, a Dutch Ministry of Defence contract, and a US Army battle-manager software award using its Lattice platform. The broader defense technology funding environment is active: Shield AI raised $1.5 billion at a $12.7 billion valuation in March, and hypersonic startup Hermeus raised $350 million at a $1 billion-plus valuation last month.

Why it mattersThe Anduril round is the clearest single data point confirming that AI-native autonomy and software-defined defense platforms are now treated by venture and institutional investors as a category comparable in scale to AI model labs. With more than $30 billion added to the company's valuation in under a year, defense procurement is being repriced around software companies rather than legacy prime contractors – and the DoD's parallel decision to fund competition across multiple new suppliers makes clear it is willing to break from its historical pattern of single large-prime contracts.

The US and China are establishing a formal bilateral AI safety protocol at the Trump–Xi Beijing summit, linking frontier model exports, semiconductor controls, and financial-sector cybersecurity into a single negotiation for the first time

US Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent told CNBC in an interview aired May 14 that the United States and China will establish a formal protocol on AI best practices and safeguards on the sidelines of the Trump–Xi summit in Beijing, with the stated goal of preventing non-state actors from gaining access to the most powerful AI models. Reuters reported the same day from Washington that both delegations are actively discussing AI guardrails. Bessent told CNBC it was "of utmost importance" that the US maintain its lead over China in AI, and attributed the ability to hold "wholesome discussions" with China to the US being ahead in model capability. He also anticipated what he called a "step-function jump" in upcoming model releases from Google and OpenAI, and named Anthropic, Google, and OpenAI as government partners.

The protocol is being negotiated in the context of Anthropic's Mythos model – whose disclosed cyber-offensive capabilities have prompted vulnerability patching across US banks. Bessent confirmed Treasury has been working with the 11 largest US banks to ensure they patch exposed vulnerabilities, and signaled the same work will extend to super-regional and community banks. Separately, Reuters reported the US has cleared approximately 10 Chinese companies to buy Nvidia's H200 chip – the second-most powerful in Nvidia's current lineup – and that Nvidia CEO Jensen Huang joined Trump's Beijing delegation as a late addition.

Why it mattersThis is the first publicly confirmed bilateral US–China AI safety mechanism, and it links three previously separate strands of policy – frontier model access, semiconductor export controls, and financial-sector cybersecurity – into a single negotiation framework. If a protocol is signed, it establishes a precedent: AI safety becomes the lever through which Washington and Beijing also negotiate chip access. If it is not signed, every other multilateral framework – G7, OECD, EU AI Act third-country provisions – becomes the fallback by default.

Palo Alto Networks disclosed 26 software vulnerabilities in a single advisory after frontier AI models scanned more than 130 of its own products – five times the company's typical monthly volume – while Microsoft separately attributed 16 Patch Tuesday findings to its own AI scanning system

Palo Alto Networks, a major US cybersecurity company, published on May 13 a security advisory disclosing 26 CVEs (Common Vulnerabilities and Exposures – the standard system used to catalog software security flaws) representing 75 individual vulnerability issues, found across a scan of more than 130 of its own products. The company stated this was the first month where the majority of findings came from frontier AI models – large AI systems of the kind made by Anthropic and OpenAI – scanning its own code, and specifically credited Anthropic's Claude Mythos, Claude Opus 4.7, and OpenAI's GPT-5.5-Cyber. The company's typical monthly advisory volume is fewer than 5 CVEs. None of the 26 CVEs are rated critical and none are reported as exploited by attackers in the wild. Three are rated high-severity: an IKEv2 remote code execution flaw, a heap buffer overflow in DNS proxy and server components, and a Cloud Authentication Service authentication bypass.

Microsoft separately disclosed that its MDASH system – an orchestration layer running more than 100 specialized AI agents across multiple frontier models – was used to find 16 vulnerabilities in this month's Patch Tuesday release. An independent analysis published the same week by security researcher Davi Ottenheimer notes a discrepancy between Palo Alto's CSV advisory feed (23 CVE-numbered entries) and its blog post (claiming 26), and raises questions about how much of the discovery represents genuinely new AI capability versus more systematic deployment of tools that have existed for years. The Palo Alto advisory landed five days after the company separately patched CVE-2026-0300, a CVSS 9.3-rated zero-day vulnerability in PAN-OS firewalls that was actively exploited and listed on CISA's Known Exploited Vulnerabilities catalog.

Why it mattersThis is the first major security vendor to publicly attribute a step-change in vulnerability discovery volume to frontier AI models, and to publish the absolute number of findings against its own product surface in a named monthly advisory. If the methodology survives peer review, every other security and infrastructure vendor will face pressure from enterprise customers to disclose what they have found by running the same models against their own code – and to do so on a Microsoft/Palo Alto cadence.

Pharma & Health Financial Services

Isomorphic Labs, the Google DeepMind spinout applying AI to drug discovery, raised a $2.1 billion Series B led by Thrive Capital and joined by two sovereign-wealth funds, bringing total capital raised to $2.6 billion despite having no compound yet dosed in a human patient

Isomorphic Labs, a London-based AI drug discovery company spun out of Google DeepMind in 2021, announced a $2.1 billion Series B financing round on May 12, 2026. The round was led by Thrive Capital – which also led Isomorphic's $600 million Series A in October 2025 – and included existing investors Alphabet (Google's parent company) and GV, joined by new investors MGX (Abu Dhabi's sovereign technology investment fund), Temasek (Singapore's state investment company), CapitalG, and the UK Sovereign AI Fund. Total capital raised since the company's founding now stands at approximately $2.6 billion. The round's valuation was not disclosed.

Capital will be used to scale the company's proprietary AI drug design engine, called IsoDDE (Isomorphic Drug Design Engine), which is built on the AlphaFold scientific foundation that earned co-founder Sir Demis Hassabis and colleague John Jumper a share of the 2024 Nobel Prize in Chemistry. AlphaFold is an AI system that can predict the three-dimensional shape of proteins – a task central to designing new drugs. The company plans to expand its internal drug pipeline toward Phase I clinical trials (the first stage of human testing) and hire across AI research, engineering, drug design, and clinical functions at offices in London, Cambridge (Massachusetts), and Lausanne. Isomorphic has existing drug discovery partnerships with Novartis, Eli Lilly, and Johnson & Johnson. No Isomorphic-discovered compound has yet been dosed in a human patient, per Analytics Drift's reporting.

Why it mattersA Series A-to-Series B in seven months at 3.5 times the previous round size is unusual for a pre-clinical biotech that has not yet dosed a patient. The entry of two sovereign-wealth funds and the UK Sovereign AI Fund signals that AI-native drug discovery is now being treated by state allocators as strategic infrastructure rather than venture biotech. If IsoDDE produces a clinical asset that reads out positively in the next 24–36 months, this round sets a template for the AI-bio category; if it does not, it becomes the largest mark in an emerging debate about whether the capability is real or the wet-lab gap has not yet been closed.

AI compute is running into physical-world limits: xAI is operating 46 unpermitted gas turbines in Mississippi, Nvidia has committed more than $40 billion to equity stakes in its own customers, and SoftBank is converting a factory into a gigawatt-scale battery plant to power AI data centers

Three separate stories from this week point at the same underlying constraint: AI compute growth is now pressing against the limits of electricity supply, permits, and grid capacity. The most acute example is in Mississippi, where the state's Department of Environmental Quality has confirmed that xAI – the AI company founded by Elon Musk – is running 46 "temporary-mobile" natural-gas turbines at its Southaven facility to power its Colossus 2 data center across the state line in Memphis. Only 15 of those turbines have the required air permits. Another 19 were added between March 25 and May 2 alone. The turbines can collectively generate up to 495 megawatts of power – the equivalent of a conventional power plant. xAI is using a Mississippi state classification that labels trailer-mounted turbines as "mobile," making them exempt from full air-pollution regulation for one year. The NAACP, represented by the Southern Environmental Law Center and Earthjustice, filed a lawsuit and asked a court for an injunction against xAI this week.

The second story is upstream capital concentration. Nvidia – the US company that makes the specialized chips used to run AI – has committed more than $40 billion to AI-related equity investments in the first five months of 2026, per CNBC's tally of FactSet data. The largest single position is a $30 billion stake in OpenAI. Nvidia also holds commitments of up to $3.2 billion in glassmaker Corning and up to $2.1 billion in data-center operator IREN. Non-marketable equity securities on Nvidia's balance sheet rose from $3.39 billion to $22.25 billion year-on-year, per SEC filings cited by CNBC. The strategic question this creates is explicit: when Nvidia's largest customers are also its largest portfolio companies, a slowdown in AI capital expenditure would hit Nvidia's income statement and balance sheet at the same time. The third story is the infrastructure response. SoftBank Corp. announced on May 11 it is launching a gigawatt-hour-scale battery business in Japan, converting a former Sharp LCD factory in Sakai City, Osaka into a manufacturing site for next-generation zinc-halogen battery cells – in partnership with South Korea's Cosmos Lab and DeltaX – with a target of gigawatt-hour-per-year scale manufacturing by approximately fiscal year 2028 and a stated revenue target of more than 100 billion yen (approximately $638 million) annually from the battery business by 2030.

Why it mattersFor a decade, "scaling AI" meant buying more chips. The next phase is winning fights over electricity, permits, and grid capacity. Whoever controls power infrastructure controls the pace of AI development. The three stories show three distinct strategies stacked simultaneously: brute-force generation behind a legal loophole; buying equity in the supply chain so that capital absorbs bottlenecks; and vertically integrating energy storage with data-center capacity. The smart capital, increasingly, is building batteries and turbines – not just buying chips.

Editor's note
Today's stories cluster around a single structural fact: AI capability is now hitting limits at both ends of the stack at the same time – at the software layer, and at the physical world.

At the software end, the model layer is compressing time and cost so aggressively that whole organizational categories are repricing themselves. Cisco substitutes payroll for AI-network capex while posting record revenue. PwC industrializes Claude as the operating layer of Fortune 500 transformation. Anduril prices AI-native autonomy as a new defense category worth $61 billion. Palo Alto Networks admits frontier models find vulnerabilities at five times the rate its previous process did. And Isomorphic Labs raises $2.1 billion to push AI from protein-structure prediction into actual clinical assets – despite having no human trials yet underway.

At the physical end, the same capability is running into electricity, permits, and grid capacity hard enough that xAI is operating 46 unpermitted gas turbines in Mississippi, Nvidia has deployed more than $40 billion buying equity in its own supply chain in five months, and SoftBank is converting an LCD factory into a gigawatt-scale battery plant explicitly to power AI. Stitched across both ends is the Bessent–Beijing AI safety protocol – the regulatory framework being negotiated to govern frontier models whose downstream consequences in cyber, energy, and biotech are already running ahead of any existing framework. The organizations most exposed by the combination are mid-market enterprises with legacy infrastructure, regulators reacting after the fact, and any company whose AI roadmap quietly assumes that compute, power, and capital will keep flowing on current terms – because at least one of the three is now visibly being rationed.
